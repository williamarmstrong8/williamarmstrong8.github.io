Prosthetic Exo-Skeleton

June 2021

We reached out to professors across the USA and partnered with a Vanderbilt professor willing to collaborate with us on this project. Weekly Zoom meetings allowed us to prototype remotely, with a site visit to Vanderbilt's engineering facilities for in-person insights. With guidance from the professor and graduate students, we developed a prototype utilizing an Arduino and a custom printed circuit board (PCB), which served as the foundation for connecting and controlling all the electronic components. The motor was driven by electromyography (EMG) sensors, which detect electrical activity in muscles, placed on the bicep and tricep of the same arm. This innovative integration allowed us to translate muscle signals into precise movements for the exoskeleton, demonstrating the functionality and potential of our design.

We were driven by a desire to expand our engineering skills beyond the foundational concepts we learned in high school. This project provided an invaluable opportunity to explore advanced topics such as complex Arduino programming, custom PCB design, and the integration of various electrical components. Along the way, we also gained a deeper understanding of the engineering design process, applying it to solve real-world challenges at an early stage in our education. Building on our knowledge of Arduino, we combined it with intricate components to create a functioning prototype. A key achievement was developing the first exoskeleton with EMG sensors integrated into the same arm as the exoskeleton itself, showcasing an innovative approach to motor control and signal processing. This integration not only improved the system's usability but also highlighted our ability to think creatively and execute technically complex solutions.

Saint Ignatius 3D Remodel

June 2021

During my time at Mark Cavagnero Associates, I had the opportunity to work on an ambitious project—a 3D model of the Saint Ignatius College Preparatory campus. Leveraging the firm's industrial-grade 3D printer, I transformed complex CAD models from Rhino and Revit into printable files using advanced CAD software. This process required completely remodeling the building to ensure precision and printability, as well as introducing an innovative approach to separate each floor of the building. This unique design allowed investors to examine each level's layout and floor plan, providing a detailed and tangible representation of the project. The final 3D-printed model not only visualized the building's scale and feasibility but also served as a critical tool for aligning the project’s vision with investor expectations.

Beyond the technical work, I immersed myself in the broader aspects of architectural and construction projects. This experience deepened my appreciation for the intersection of design, engineering, and construction and highlighted the impact of tangible models in bringing visionary ideas to life.

Motorized Projector Arm

April 2020

This project was a passion project I took on at home. I always wanted to learn how to use motors and circuits, so what better way than to dive into YouTube tutorials and experiment with an Arduino kit? I salvaged parts from a broken 3D printer, including a stepper motor, and combined them with an RFID sensor and a remote to control this projector.

The custom design was tailored for the sunroof in my room. When not in use, the projector arm would retract above the ceiling, blending seamlessly with the surrounding space. With a simple remote command, the stepper motor extended the arm to the perfect height and angle to project onto my wall, creating a fully immersive viewing experience.

This project was a fantastic introduction to the world of circuitry and engineering. Building something completely from scratch taught me invaluable lessons about problem-solving, resourcefulness, and leveraging free tools and tutorials. It remains one of my favorite passion projects, as it combined functionality, creativity, and learning into one rewarding experience.

Controlled-Access Refrigeration Insert

September 2024

This project involved designing and creating a refrigeration insert with controlled access, specifically for individuals with Prader-Willi Syndrome (PWS). PWS is a genetic condition that causes an uncontrollable urge to eat, making food access a safety concern. The goal was to develop an easy-to-use, secure system that uses RFID technology to regulate food access at specific times, helping users maintain their independence and health.

The system includes a lock controlled by solenoids, RFID wristbands for user identification, and a timer to set access schedules. To ensure safety and usability, the design addressed challenges like preventing tampering, reducing caregiver stress, and being user-friendly. The first prototype was made from 3mm acrylic to test the concept, with plans to use stronger materials like sheet metal and custom electronics for future versions.

Testing showed that the prototype successfully controlled food access and highlighted areas for improvement, such as making the structure stronger and refining the electronic components. For example, testing the lock’s resistance to tampering revealed the force range (4–18 kN) needed to prevent break-ins. This project is a significant step toward a practical solution that enhances safety and promotes independence for those with PWS.
